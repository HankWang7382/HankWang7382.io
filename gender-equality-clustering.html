01 · Overview

Abstract

Research Background & Objective

The Gender Equality Evaluation Model classifies TWSE-listed and TPEx-listed companies into peer groups based on their female workforce structure, using hierarchical clustering to enable fair comparison of gender-related performance indicators. As more companies submit ESG reports each year, new data points enter each review cycle, potentially altering cluster assignments and reducing the model's year-over-year consistency.

This study tests a range of algorithmic settings to identify which combination best maintains clustering stability over time. Five linkage methods, three cluster validity indices, two outlier detection approaches, and various cluster count constraints are evaluated across six annual review cycles from 2016 to 2021, with F-measure derived from confusion matrices serving as the primary stability indicator.

02 · Research Methods

Hierarchical Clustering

Framework Overview · Configuration Variables

Hierarchical clustering constructs a dendrogram by repeatedly merging the two closest clusters until all observations belong to a single group. In this study, the female employee ratio — averaged over the preceding two years — is used as the sole clustering variable. Two types of settings are varied: the linkage method, which defines how distance between clusters is measured, and the cluster validity index, which determines the optimal number of clusters. The goal is to find the combination that produces the most consistent cluster assignments from one annual review to the next.

Once the best algorithmic pairing is identified, four additional variables are tested one at a time: whether to include an industry-based split-group pre-processing step, whether to compress outlier values before clustering, which outlier detection method to apply, and how to set the upper and lower bounds on the number of clusters. Each variable is assessed for its independent effect on stability.

02.1 · Inter-cluster Distance

Linkage Methods

Five methods tested · Euclidean distance as base measure

Single-linkage
The distance between two clusters is defined as the distance between their nearest pair of points. This method is prone to chaining, where clusters grow by absorbing nearby points one at a time, often resulting in highly uneven cluster sizes.
d(Cᵢ, Cⱼ) = mina∈Cᵢ, b∈Cⱼ d(a, b)
Complete-linkage
The distance between two clusters is defined as the distance between their most distant pair of points. This approach tends to form compact clusters of roughly equal size.
d(Cᵢ, Cⱼ) = maxa∈Cᵢ, b∈Cⱼ d(a, b)
Average-linkage (UPGMA)
The distance between two clusters is the mean of all pairwise distances between points from each cluster, providing a balanced measure between the extremes of single and complete linkage.
d(Cᵢ, Cⱼ) = (1 / |Cᵢ||Cⱼ|) · Σ d(a, b)  for a∈Cᵢ, b∈Cⱼ
Centroid Method
The distance between two clusters is measured between their centroids. In certain edge cases, this method can reverse the order of merges in the dendrogram, making interpretation less reliable.
d(Cᵢ, Cⱼ) = d(μ(Cᵢ), μ(Cⱼ))
Ward's Method (D2) Selected
Two clusters are merged based on the resulting increase in total within-cluster variance. By minimising variance at each step, this method consistently produces well-balanced, internally homogeneous clusters — making it the most stable option across all configurations tested in this study.
d(Cᵢ, Cⱼ) = Σ ‖a − μ(Cᵢ∪Cⱼ)‖²  for a∈Cᵢ∪Cⱼ
μ(Cᵢ∪Cⱼ): centroid of the merged cluster
02.2 · Cluster Validity Indices

Determining the Optimal Number of Clusters

Objective · Minimise intra-cluster variance · Maximise inter-cluster separation

At each review cycle, a validity index scores each candidate cluster count and selects the partition that best balances compactness within clusters and separation between them. How the index responds to small changes in cluster count has a direct bearing on how consistently the same number of clusters is chosen from one year to the next.

Average Silhouette
Measures both how tightly grouped each point is within its own cluster and how well it is separated from neighbouring clusters. A higher score indicates a better partition. Because the final score is averaged across all points, the index is sensitive to small boundary shifts between clusters.
s(i) = (b(i)−a(i)) / max(a(i), b(i))
Davies-Bouldin Index
Evaluates clustering quality by comparing intra-cluster scatter against inter-cluster distance. A lower value indicates a better result. Like Silhouette, it relies on averaging, which makes it susceptible to similar instability when the cluster count changes.
DB = (1/k) Σ maxj≠i (sᵢ+sⱼ)/dᵢⱼ
Dunn Index Selected
Compares the smallest gap between any two clusters against the largest spread within any single cluster. A higher value reflects better separation and compactness. Crucially, no averaging is involved in the calculation, which makes the index far more consistent in its cluster-count rankings across review cycles.
DI = min δ(Cᵢ,Cⱼ) / max Δₙ
Why Dunn Index is more stable: Both Silhouette and Davies-Bouldin involve averaging across all clusters or data points. When the cluster count changes by just one, a single poorly-formed cluster can drag down the overall score noticeably, causing the optimal cluster count ordering to shift erratically from year to year — an effect that is especially pronounced under Single-linkage. The Dunn Index sidesteps this issue entirely by relying only on minimum and maximum values, leading to a more predictable priority ordering of candidate cluster counts across review cycles.
02.3 · Pre-processing Step

Split-group Method

Industry-level Segmentation · Separating Structurally Different Sub-groups

Companies within the same TWSE and TPEx industry classification can vary considerably in their female workforce composition. The transportation sector, for instance, brings together both shipping companies — where the workforce tends to be predominantly male — and aviation companies, which typically employ a higher proportion of female staff. Placing these structurally different firms in the same peer group without prior separation would compromise the fairness of any subsequent indicator comparisons.

The split-group method addresses this issue by performing a preliminary clustering step within each TWSE and TPEx industry, using female employee ratio as the basis for further subdivision. Single-linkage is applied at this stage, as it is most effective at isolating sub-groups with noticeably different female workforce profiles from one another.

Splitting is triggered when the spread of female ratios within an industry exceeds a defined dispersion threshold, and continues until each resulting sub-cluster satisfies internal homogeneity requirements — including a sufficient number of members and the absence of outliers as identified by the IQR box-plot method. Sub-clusters consisting of only one company are excluded from further splitting.

Note on research findings: Although the split-group step is well-grounded in theory, the data shows that omitting it actually leads to better clustering outcomes and higher F-measure stability. Without the industry constraint, companies are grouped freely based on the similarity of their female workforce ratios alone, resulting in more natural and internally consistent clusters. A full comparison is provided in Section 04.1.
02.4 · Clustering Pipeline

Merge-group Method

Aggregation Process · Grouping Companies by Female Workforce Profile

In the main clustering stage, companies are grouped directly by their female employee ratio. The validity index scores each candidate cluster count from best to worst, and the algorithm adopts the first count that meets the internal quality criteria as the final partition for that review cycle. A defined minimum and maximum cluster count constrains the search; if no count within this range fully satisfies the criteria, the boundary value is used as a fallback.

The quality criteria focus on two aspects: each cluster must contain enough members to be representative, and the spread of values within each cluster must not be unduly wide. These thresholds are set to ensure that the resulting groups are sufficiently homogeneous for meaningful cross-group indicator comparisons in subsequent stages of the evaluation.

Key design decision: The cluster count range is one of the most consequential parameters for long-term stability. Widening the upper bound beyond the optimal range was found to introduce inconsistency in year-over-year cluster assignments without meaningfully improving average F-measure. Based on the analysis, the final configuration limits the search to between 4 and 9 clusters.

03 · Stability Metric

Measuring Cluster Consistency with Confusion Matrix & F-measure

Law of Comparative Judgment · Pairwise Clustering · Precision · Recall · F-measure (β=1)

To measure year-over-year clustering stability, the current cycle's partition is treated as the predicted clustering (P), while the previous cycle's partition serves as the gold standard (Q). Stability is assessed by how well P reproduces Q among the companies that took part in both review cycles.

Why use a confusion matrix with pairwise comparisons? Drawing on the Law of Comparative Judgment, this study evaluates clustering similarity through pairs of clusterings rather than by directly matching cluster labels. For every pair of companies in the shared sample, the question is simply: were they placed in the same cluster in both P and Q, or not? This pairwise approach avoids the label-alignment problem that arises in hierarchical clustering, since cluster identifiers are inherently arbitrary and change between cycles. A confusion matrix is then built from these pairwise outcomes, and F-measure is used to quantify how accurately the predicted clustering (P) reproduces the gold-standard clustering (Q).
Confusion Matrix Mapping
TP (a): pair grouped together in both P and Q
FP (c): together in P, but separated in Q
FN (b): separated in P, but together in Q
TN (d): separated in both P and Q
Metrics
Precision = a / (a + c)
Recall = a / (a + b)
F-measure = 2a / (2a + b + c)

Why not rely on Precision or Recall alone? Each metric captures a different dimension of clustering similarity, and optimising either one independently leads to a flawed outcome.

Chasing high Precision — where every pair predicted by P also appears in Q — encourages the algorithm to break clusters into smaller, more specific sub-groups. Although each predicted pairing becomes more reliable, many pairs from Q are never captured, driving up FN. The practical consequence is that some clusters end up with too few members, limiting their usefulness for comparative indicator analysis.

Chasing high Recall — where every pair in Q is also captured by P — pushes the algorithm in the opposite direction, merging clusters aggressively to ensure nothing is missed. While FN drops, many predicted pairs have no counterpart in Q (high FP), resulting in oversized clusters that bring together companies with very different female workforce profiles, which undermines the fairness of the evaluation.

F-measure (β=1) treats both Precision and Recall as equally important. It rewards configurations that maintain meaningful cluster sizes and internal similarity simultaneously — the two qualities most essential for reliable, fair indicator evaluation across review cycles. An Adjusted F-measure = Average F-measure ÷ SD F-measure is also reported, jointly rewarding high average stability and low year-to-year variation.

04 · Results

Stability Analysis Across Configurations

Four Variables Tested · Impact on F-measure · 2016–2021

Each variable is examined in turn, with all other conditions held constant. The reference point for comparison is a configuration that omits the split-group step, applies no outlier compression, uses IQR-based outlier detection, and constrains the cluster count to a range of 4–10.

04.1 · Variable: Split-group Step

Impact of the Split-group Pre-processing Step

With vs. Without Industry-level Segmentation

When the split-group step is applied, companies within each TWSE and TPEx industry are first divided by female employee ratio before being passed to the main clustering stage. This pre-processing can usefully separate sub-industries with distinct workforce compositions, but it also limits how companies can be reassigned during merging — each company can only join clusters formed from within its own industry partition.

Removing the split-group step allows every company to be clustered directly based on female employee ratio, regardless of industry. This gives the algorithm greater freedom to form groups that reflect genuine similarity in workforce structure across sector boundaries.

Conclusion: Removing the split-group step improves both clustering validity and F-measure stability. All subsequent analyses use the no-split-group setting as the reference configuration.
04.2 · Variable: Outlier Compression

Impact of Compressing Outlier Values

Winsorising IQR-detected Outliers to Boundary Values

Outlier compression, also known as winsorisation, replaces extreme female employee ratio values — those flagged as outliers by the IQR box-plot method — with the corresponding boundary value (Q1 − 1.5×IQR or Q3 + 1.5×IQR) before clustering. Rather than removing these companies from the sample, their values are capped so that they remain in the analysis without distorting the cluster structure.

With Compression
Avg. F = 0.630 · Adj. F = 7.97
A slight improvement in both average and adjusted F-measure compared to the baseline without compression.
Without Compression (Baseline)
Avg. F = 0.628 · Adj. F = 7.83
The gap between the two conditions is negligible; both achieve the clustering goal across all configurations.
Conclusion: Outlier compression produces only a marginal improvement and does not justify the added processing step. The no-compression baseline is kept for subsequent analyses.
04.3 · Variable: Outlier Detection Method

IQR Box-plot vs. Standard Deviation

Comparing Two Outlier Identification Approaches

Two approaches to identifying outliers are compared. The IQR box-plot method flags values that fall below Q1 − 1.5×IQR or above Q3 + 1.5×IQR. The standard deviation method flags values that lie more than ±2 SD from the mean. Because the distribution of female employee ratios across industries is often skewed rather than symmetric, the two methods can produce quite different outlier sets.

IQR Box-plot ★ Selected
Avg. F = 0.628 · Adj. F = 7.83

±2 SD Threshold

Conclusion: The IQR method proves considerably more reliable for this dataset. The SD threshold is too permissive in the presence of skewed distributions, allowing more extreme ratio values into the clusters and causing certain configurations to fall short of the quality criteria. IQR is adopted for all remaining analyses.
04.4 · Variable: Cluster Count Range

Impact of Maximum & Minimum Cluster Count

Constraining the Search Space · Kendall's Tau Stability Analysis

The cluster count range defines the set of candidate values from which the validity index selects the optimal partition. A wider range offers the algorithm more options, but also increases the risk of large year-to-year swings in cluster count, which in turn destabilises F-measure. A narrower range reduces this variability but at the cost of flexibility.

Kendall's tau is used to assess how consistently the validity index ranks candidate cluster counts from one review cycle to the next. A high value indicates that the priority ordering of cluster counts remains largely the same across years — meaning the algorithm reliably converges on a similar number of clusters each time.

05 · Conclusion

Optimal Configuration & Research Summary

Synthesising Findings Across All Four Variables

Four methodological variables were tested in this study — the split-group pre-processing step, outlier compression, the choice of outlier detection method, and the cluster count range. Each was assessed independently, and together their results point clearly towards a single best-performing configuration.

The split-group step, while theoretically well-motivated as a way to handle industry heterogeneity, was found to limit the algorithm's flexibility and consistently prevented the clustering quality criteria from being fully met. Once removed, companies could be grouped freely based on the similarity of their female workforce ratios, without being constrained by industry boundaries. This produced better-defined, more stable clusters.

Among the 12 linkage–index combinations tested, Ward's method paired with the Dunn Index delivered the highest adjusted F-measure across all cluster count ranges. Ward's method promotes balanced, homogeneous clusters by minimising within-cluster variance at each step. The Dunn Index, which involves no averaging in its formula, avoids the instability that causes Silhouette and Davies-Bouldin to produce erratic cluster-count orderings — particularly when used together with Single-linkage.

For outlier detection, the IQR box-plot method proved more dependable than the standard deviation threshold. The SD approach is too permissive when data is skewed, allowing more extreme female ratio values into the clusters and causing several configurations to fail their quality checks. The IQR method handles asymmetric distributions more reliably.

Constraining the cluster count to a range of 4–9 achieves the best balance between consistency and quality. This range produces the lowest year-to-year SD (0.030) and the highest adjusted F-measure (20.65). Extending the upper bound beyond 9 introduces instability — the extra flexibility allows cluster counts to shift more dramatically between cycles — without delivering a meaningful improvement in average F-measure.

Final Configuration Summary

  • Group companies directly by female employee ratio — omit the TWSE and TPEx industry split-group pre-processing step
  • Linkage method: Ward's (D2) — minimises within-cluster variance at each merge step; performs most consistently as the sample size grows over time
  • Cluster validity index: Dunn Index — no averaging involved in the formula; delivers the most stable cluster-count priority ordering from one review cycle to the next
  • Outlier detection: IQR box-plot method — better suited to the skewed distribution of female employee ratios than the ±2 SD threshold
  • Cluster count range: minimum 4, maximum 9 — lowest year-to-year SD of 0.030; adjusted F-measure of 20.65 across the 2016–2021 review period
